Plastic Pollution & Marine Debris

Plastic pollution is one of the most visible and persistent threats to marine ecosystems. From large lost fishing nets to microscopic fragments, plastics enter the ocean from land and sea-based sources and persist for decades — threatening wildlife, food safety, and coastal economies.

Sources & pathways

Plastic reaches the ocean through rivers, coastal littering, stormwater runoff, shipping, and fishing activities. Major sources include single-use packaging, fishing gear, microplastics from personal care products and synthetic textiles, and improper waste management systems.

Impacts on wildlife and people

  • Entanglement and ingestion: marine animals can become entangled or ingest plastic, causing injury or death.
  • Microplastics in food webs: tiny plastic fragments are ingested by plankton and move up the food chain.
  • Toxic transport: plastics can concentrate and transport pollutants.
  • Economic costs: cleanup, lost tourism, and impacts on fisheries.
Community action makes a difference

Local beach cleanups reduce the immediate risk to wildlife and raise awareness. Data collected during cleanups helps identify sources and informs local policy on waste management and single-use reductions.

What governments & communities can do

  • Improve waste collection and recycling systems.
  • Ban or reduce single-use plastics and promote alternatives.
  • Support innovations for plastic capture in rivers and stormwater.
  • Invest in public education and citizen science monitoring programs.

How to reduce your plastic footprint

Simple daily choices add up:

  • Bring reusable bags, bottles, and containers.
  • Avoid single-use cutlery, straws, and packaging when possible.
  • Choose products with minimal plastic packaging.
  • Participate in or organize local clean-ups and data collection.

Quick facts

An estimated 8 million tonnes of plastic enter the oceans every year. Microplastics have been found in salt, seafood, and even drinking water.
