Overfishing

Overfishing occurs when fish and other marine species are caught at a faster rate than populations can replenish. This practice threatens biodiversity, undermines food security, and damages livelihoods that depend on healthy fisheries.

Causes

  • Excessive fishing effort driven by high demand and subsidies.
  • Illegal, unreported, and unregulated (IUU) fishing.
  • Destructive gear (bottom trawling, dynamite fishing) that damages habitats.
  • Lack of effective management or enforcement in many regions.

Consequences

When key species are overfished, ecosystems become unbalanced. Predatory fish decline, prey populations may surge or collapse, and habitats such as seagrass beds and coral reefs can degrade. Coastal communities that rely on fisheries for food and income are particularly vulnerable.

Atlantic Cod (Gadus morhua)

Overfishing of Atlantic cod in the northwest Atlantic led to a dramatic population collapse in the early 1990s. Fishing moratoria and management reforms were introduced, but recovery has been slow and demonstrates how long-term depletion disrupts ecosystems and local economies.

Sustainable alternatives

Transitioning to sustainable fisheries and consumption helps protect stocks and communities:

  • Adopt and support science-based catch limits and monitoring.
  • Encourage gear modifications that reduce bycatch and habitat damage.
  • Support fisheries certified by credible standards (e.g., MSC) where appropriate.
  • Promote small-scale, community-led fisheries management and marine protected areas (MPAs).

How to choose seafood

Look for local guidance and checklists from NGOs and authorities. Avoid species known to be overfished in your region.
